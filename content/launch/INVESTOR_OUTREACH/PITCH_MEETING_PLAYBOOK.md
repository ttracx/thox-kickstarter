# PITCH_MEETING_PLAYBOOK.md

How to run an investor pitch meeting for THOX.ai LLC. Covers three meeting
lengths (15-min discovery, 30-min deep-dive, 60-min hot-seat), what to
send in advance, what to ask the investor, how to follow up, common
objections, and term-sheet red flags.

Brand: #0B1220 / #F2F4F8 / #27E5FF / #FF3DA8. IBM Plex Sans + JetBrains
Mono.

Voice: technical, honest, no marketing fluff. No fabricated investor
quotes.

Companion docs: `INVESTOR_TARGET_LIST.md`, `INVESTOR_SCREENING_FRAMEWORK.md`,
`WARM_INTRO_BUILDER.md`, `README.md`.

Last refreshed against `../INVESTOR_DECK.md` on 2026-06-25.

## Pre-meeting checklist

48 hours before the meeting:

- [ ] Re-read the candidate's screening score (per
      `INVESTOR_SCREENING_FRAMEWORK.md`) so the pitch lands on their thesis
- [ ] Re-read the partner's last 5 tweets and last published article
- [ ] Send the partner the pre-read package (see "What to send in
      advance" below)
- [ ] Confirm meeting time, video link or address, and time zone
- [ ] Send a calendar invite with the pre-read attached (do not assume
      the partner pulled the pre-read from email)

24 hours before the meeting:

- [ ] Re-read `INVESTOR_DECK.md` end-to-end
- [ ] Re-read the partner's portfolio page and pick 2 companies you'd
      like to ask about (this signals you did the homework)
- [ ] Print a backup paper copy of `INVESTOR_DECK_ONE_PAGER.pdf` (if
      meeting is in-person)
- [ ] Test the demo video link on the device you'll present from (if the
      meeting is video; do NOT screenshare from an unfamiliar device)

1 hour before the meeting:

- [ ] Charge laptop / phone
- [ ] Close all non-essential tabs and apps (avoid notification leaks
      during screenshare)
- [ ] Open `INVESTOR_DECK.pdf` (rendered version) ready to present
- [ ] Open the THOX repo graph (github.com/ttracx) in a separate tab

## What to send in advance

Three pieces of pre-read, in this exact order:

1. `INVESTOR_DECK_ONE_PAGER.pdf` (1 page, 8KB). Sent with the meeting
   invite. This is the only material the partner is GUARANTEED to read.
2. `INVESTOR_DECK.pdf` (10 slides + speaker notes, ~27KB). Linked from
   the calendar invite description. Optional read.
3. A 60-second demo video link (TBD; placeholder for when the
   `content/launch/VIDEO_SCRIPT.md` short cut is produced). Optional
   read.

Do NOT send the full data room before the meeting. Save the data room
for the post-meeting follow-up; that triggers a second engagement loop.

Do NOT send a separate "executive summary" doc. The one-pager IS the
executive summary.

Do NOT include sensitive financials (specific dollar ask, pre-money
valuation, cap table) in the pre-read. Save for the meeting itself.

## 15-minute discovery meeting format

Use when: first meeting with a partner who has not yet seen any THOX
material. Default format for warm-intro first-touch meetings.

Two structural variations: founder-led and investor-led. Confirm with
the partner which variation they prefer; default to investor-led if the
partner does not specify.

### Founder-led 15-minute format

| Minute | Section | What to do |
|---|---|---|
| 0-1 | Opener | Thank the warm-intro source. Confirm what the partner has read in advance. |
| 1-3 | Frame | One paragraph: "THOX is a family of small AI hardware devices that run inference on hardware in your hands. Four SKUs from $39 to $499 on a common Apache-2.0 runtime. Kickstarter Aug 12. Goal $250K. Ceiling $3M." |
| 3-6 | Problem + Why now | Slide 2 + Slide 5 condensed. Cloud AI's three structural costs. Edge silicon is cheap enough now. |
| 6-9 | Product + Traction | Slide 4 + Slide 6 condensed. Four SKUs. What has shipped to public repos. |
| 9-11 | Business model + Team | Slide 7 + Slide 9 condensed. Hardware-first, EDU bulk, enterprise white-label. Two co-founders, both inventors of record. |
| 11-13 | Ask | Slide 10. Hand the partner the actual ask: amount, equity, pre-money, use of funds. |
| 13-15 | Q&A | Let the partner ask 1 to 2 questions. End on time. |

### Investor-led 15-minute format

If the partner prefers investor-led:

| Minute | What to do |
|---|---|
| 0-1 | Opener as above |
| 1-15 | Answer the partner's questions in their order. Have the deck open as visual reference but do not walk through it slide-by-slide. |

Most partners will ask: what is it, why now, who is the team, what's the
ask, what's the round shape. Be ready to answer all five in any order.

### What to ask the investor (15-min version)

End the meeting with 1 to 2 questions, NEVER more:

- "What's your typical decision process from first meeting to term
  sheet?"
- "Who else on your team would want to see this, and what would the
  next step look like?"

Do NOT ask "what do you think?" -- a 15-minute meeting does not give a
partner enough to render a verdict, and asking forces them to either
say "I need to discuss with the team" (uninformative) or pre-commit
(awkward).

### Common outcomes after 15 minutes

- "Send the data room and let's schedule a 60-minute follow-up." This
  is the strongest positive signal. Send the data room within 24 hours.
- "Send the deck and I'll circulate with the team." Positive signal.
  Send the full deck PDF within 24 hours.
- "This is interesting but probably not for us right now." Soft pass.
  Thank them, ask for feedback, and log in the tracker. Do not push.
- "Not a fit for our thesis." Hard pass. Thank them, ask which firms
  they think WOULD be a fit, and log in the tracker.

## 30-minute deep-dive meeting format

Use when: second meeting after a positive 15-min discovery, or first
meeting with a partner who explicitly asked for 30 minutes.

| Minute | Section |
|---|---|
| 0-2 | Opener + re-frame for any new context since last touch |
| 2-15 | Walk through Slides 1 through 6 (frame, problem, vision, product, why now, traction) |
| 15-25 | Walk through Slides 7 through 10 (business model, competition, team, ask) |
| 25-30 | Q&A and discussion of next steps |

Variation: if the partner explicitly says "I've read the deck; talk to
me about [X]" -- skip the slide walk and go directly to X. Have the
deck open as backup but lead with conversation.

### What to ask the investor (30-min version)

End with 2 to 3 questions:

- "What's your typical decision process from first meeting to term
  sheet, and who else needs to see this on your side?"
- "What concerns would you want addressed before a term sheet?"
- "If you wrote a check, what's a typical check size at our stage for
  your fund?"

The check-size question is the only acceptable place to ask about
specific money. Do NOT lead with it.

## 60-minute hot-seat meeting format

Use when: final partner meeting before a term-sheet decision, or
full-partnership meeting where the partner has already advocated
internally.

This meeting is rarely founder-led; it is a Q&A format where the partner
(or partnership) drives. Prepare to answer questions in any order across
the entire deck plus the data room.

| Minute | Section |
|---|---|
| 0-3 | Opener + thanks |
| 3-15 | Founder background and team story (each co-founder gets ~5 minutes) |
| 15-35 | Product deep-dive: live demo if possible (have a working device on the table for in-person; have a recorded demo + repo tour for video) |
| 35-50 | Business and financials Q&A |
| 50-58 | Open Q&A on anything else (risk, competition, timing, exit thinking) |
| 58-60 | Next steps |

### Pre-60-min preparation

- Have a working ThoxMini or ThoxAir on the table for in-person
  demos
- Have the recorded 60s demo plus a 30s repo tour ready to screenshare
  for video meetings
- Pre-load github.com/ttracx in a tab and be ready to navigate to:
  - thoxcore (router + 7 adapters)
  - thox-kickstarter (campaign content + integration repo + outreach)
  - thox-quickstart (provisioner + QA matrix)
  - thox-3dprint-kit (MagStack + concept enclosures)
- Have the data room link ready to share live if requested

### What to ask the investor (60-min version)

End with 3 to 4 questions:

- "What's the next step from here, and what's the timeline you're
  thinking?"
- "Who else needs to weigh in before this becomes a term sheet?"
- "If there are concerns I haven't addressed, what would you want to
  see between now and then?"
- "Have you discussed THOX with other firms in your circle? If yes,
  who?"

## Common objections + responses

### "Hardware is hard / capital intensive / low margin."

Honest response:

> Hardware-margin businesses are unit-economics businesses, not
> subscription businesses. We do not claim hardware margins approximate
> SaaS margins. We do claim that owning the hardware layer plus an
> Apache-2.0 software stack on a common runtime is a more defensible
> moat than a software-only edge-AI play. The capital intensity is
> bracketed by the Kickstarter manufacturing run, which is the
> single-largest input to the 18-month plan. The round funds the
> operational capacity to deliver that run plus the next two.

Do NOT respond:

- "We're not really a hardware company, we're a software company."
  (Dishonest and contradicts the pitch.)
- "Hardware margins are getting better." (Vague; partner will press for
  specifics we cannot ground.)

### "Why won't a cloud AI vendor crush this with a hardware launch?"

Honest response:

> A cloud AI vendor cannot match our privacy story without abandoning
> their data-collection business model. Their hardware would still
> phone home, train on prompts, and lock the user into the vendor's
> account. Our category is the workloads where one of those three costs
> (privacy exposure, recurring cost, vendor lock-in) is unacceptable.
> We do not need to win every workload to win this category. The
> structural advantage is durable as long as the cloud AI vendors'
> business model requires data collection.

### "What's the customer LTV story?"

Honest response:

> Hardware-as-category builds LTV through upgrade and refresh, not
> monthly recurring. A buyer who starts at ThoxClip is one upgrade
> away from ThoxNova; the common runtime makes that upgrade frictionless.
> The THOXKey EDU bulk channel adds annual-refresh recurring on the
> education side. We do not project specific LTV figures in the deck
> because the Aug 12 campaign result is the single largest input to
> the model, and we update the deck post-campaign.

### "What if the Kickstarter doesn't hit goal?"

Honest response:

> The technology and the supply work are de-risked independent of the
> campaign result. The campaign is the manufacturing capital event,
> not the moment we start being a real company. The 30+ public repos
> are the technical due-diligence package. We've sized the round to
> underwrite the manufacturing run regardless of where the campaign
> lands inside the $250K to $3M envelope. A miss on goal is a delay
> on the manufacturing run, not an existential event.

### "Why are you only raising $X? Other founders at your stage are
raising $10M."

Honest response:

> We are raising the amount required to do the work in the deck. We
> are not raising more to inflate the valuation or to extend our cap-
> table optionality. Over-raising at pre-seed compresses founder equity
> through dilution and pressures the next round to a milestone the team
> does not need to hit to be successful. We'd rather raise the right
> amount now and raise a priced Series A from a position of strength
> post-campaign.

### "Tell me about your customer acquisition cost."

Honest response:

> Pre-campaign, CAC is dominated by the Kickstarter platform's organic
> backer base plus our pre-launch list. Post-campaign, CAC shifts to a
> mix of EDU bulk channel (lowest CAC), retail / e-commerce (medium
> CAC), and direct outreach for enterprise white-label (highest CAC
> per unit but highest ASP). We do not have a CAC number to share until
> the campaign produces one. We commit to a CAC discipline post-
> campaign: we will not chase paid customer acquisition that we cannot
> sustain on hardware margin.

### "Who are you afraid of?"

Honest response:

> A single-device edge AI competitor with a head-start on
> certifications could squeeze our launch window. A cloud AI vendor
> repositioning to a hybrid on-device tier could blur our category
> story. Neither has happened yet. Our mitigation: ship the four SKUs
> on time, harden the certifications inside the round's funding window,
> and let the Apache-2.0 community lead the developer narrative.

## How to follow up

Always follow up within 24 hours of any meeting, regardless of outcome.

### After a positive meeting

Email template:

> Subject: Following up after our meeting
>
> Hi {partner_first_name},
>
> Thanks for the time today. As discussed, attached / linked:
>
> - Data room: {link}
> - Full deck PDF (re-attached for reference)
> - {any specific document the partner asked for}
>
> The next step from our side: {specific action: e.g., "we'll respond
> to your team's diligence questions within 48 hours of receipt" or
> "we'll schedule the follow-up partnership meeting per your team's
> availability"}.
>
> One question we did not get to: {one open question from the meeting
> the partner raised but we did not fully answer}. Our position is
> {one-paragraph follow-up}.
>
> Best,
> Tommy

### After a soft pass

Email template:

> Subject: Thanks for the conversation
>
> Hi {partner_first_name},
>
> Appreciated the time and the honest read on the fit. Two questions if
> you have a moment:
>
> 1. Is there anything we could change about the pitch that would
>    revisit the fit for your fund in 6 to 12 months?
> 2. Are there 1 to 2 funds you'd recommend we engage given our category
>    and stage?
>
> No pressure on either.
>
> Best,
> Tommy

### After a hard pass

Email template:

> Subject: Thanks for the meeting
>
> Hi {partner_first_name},
>
> Appreciated the candor. We'll be in touch if our story changes in a
> direction that revisits the fit.
>
> Best,
> Tommy

### After no reply within 7 days

ONE polite follow-up only:

> Subject: Quick follow-up
>
> Hi {partner_first_name},
>
> Following up on my note from {date}. Happy to wait if the timing's
> off; just wanted to make sure it didn't get buried.
>
> Best,
> Tommy

If no reply within another 7 days, log as "no reply, deprioritized" and
move on. Do not send a third follow-up. Do not re-engage for 90 days
minimum.

## Term-sheet red flags

When a term sheet arrives, the operator forwards it to THOX's startup
attorney for review. Do NOT counter-sign anything before attorney review.

These red flags require immediate attorney consultation:

1. **Full ratchet anti-dilution.** Standard for THOX's stage is broad-
   based weighted average. Full ratchet means a down-round wipes founder
   ownership disproportionately.

2. **Founder-vesting acceleration carve-outs.** Standard is double-
   trigger acceleration (acquisition plus termination without cause).
   Any clause that strips acceleration in specific scenarios is a red
   flag.

3. **Mandatory super-pro-rata.** Standard is pro-rata up to current
   ownership percentage. Super-pro-rata (above current ownership) at
   the lead's option is a red flag because it caps the round's
   participation list for future rounds.

4. **MFN clauses without expiration.** MFN (most-favored-nation) clauses
   that survive past the next priced round complicate every future
   round.

5. **Right of first refusal on follow-on rounds.** Standard is pro-rata
   right. ROFR on follow-on rounds gives the lead a veto on future
   investors.

6. **Information rights at seed stage that exceed standard NVCA.**
   Quarterly financials + annual budget is standard. Monthly financials,
   monthly board updates, monthly investor calls, or any audit right at
   the lead's discretion is a red flag.

7. **Board seat with veto rights at seed.** A board seat is reasonable
   at seed; a board seat WITH veto rights on hiring, firing, fundraising,
   or product decisions is a red flag.

8. **Founder employment agreement that locks salary or non-compete
   beyond standard.** Standard is fair-market salary at the company's
   stage with standard 1-year non-compete. Any deviation requires
   attorney review.

9. **Cap-table cleanup demands that hurt prior contributors.** The lead
   may demand option-pool reshuffling that washes out advisor or
   employee equity granted before the round. This is sometimes
   reasonable; the operator must review with attorney.

10. **Closing conditions tied to specific Kickstarter result.** A
    closing condition that requires THOX to hit a specific campaign
    number (e.g., "round closes only if Kickstarter exceeds $1M") is
    a misalignment of incentives. The round and the campaign are
    complementary, not substitutes.

If any of the above appears, the operator's response is: "Thanks for
the term sheet. Let me review with our attorney and come back to you
within {N} business days." Do NOT signal alarm or rejection during the
first review pass; the attorney's read is what matters.

## Anti-patterns

- Walking through 10 slides in 15 minutes. The partner will tune out.
  Condense.
- Reading the speaker notes verbatim. The notes are presenter prep, not
  a script.
- Going over the meeting time. Ending at minute 17 of a 15-minute slot
  signals you do not respect time. End at minute 14 with a clean handoff.
- Sending the data room before the meeting. The data room is the
  post-meeting engagement loop, not pre-read.
- Pushing for a verdict in the meeting. Partners decide between
  meetings, not in them.
- Disclosing the cap table or specific ask on the first 15-minute
  meeting unless explicitly asked. Disclose on the follow-up.
- Following up more than once on a no-reply. Move on after one polite
  bump.
- Promising deliverables to the partner inside the meeting that you
  cannot ship inside 48 hours. Under-promise, over-deliver.
